## **Foundational Principles of Ayurvedic Science**

An effective practitioner should encompass wisdom and insight to shed
light on a patient\'s internal health, which is key to tackling
ailments. An accurate assessment is fundamental to achieving successful
treatment outcomes.

The Ayurvedic diagnostic approach starts with identifying an
individual's constitutional type---known as Prakriti. Recent genomic
studies have backed the biomedical validity of this constitutional
evaluation. Health is viewed as a personalized, relative state, and
understanding one\'s Prakriti helps clarify why some individuals get
certain diseases while others do not. By determining Prakriti---based on
a mix of psychophysical traits and sociocultural
influences---practitioners can guide each person towards achieving their
best health. Patients need to follow personalized advice from doctors to
attain and maintain their ideal health.

Ayurvedic philosophy teaches that everything in the universe is made up
of five eternal elements, collectively known as Panchamahabhoothas:
earth, air, fire, water, and space. Humans are seen as microcosms of
this universal structure. These five elements, combined with
consciousness, create living beings. Each element exhibits unique traits
and exists in our bodies as Thridoshas---Vatha, Pitha, and Kapha. The
focus of Ayurvedic wisdom is to balance these three fundamental forces
to harmonize body, mind, and spirit.

Just as the three doshas make up our physical being, three mental
qualities---Satva, Rajas, and Tamas---form the core of our psychological
makeup. Everyone has different proportions of these qualities, with the
dominant trait shaping their mental state. In addition, there are seven
Dhatus (body tissues) that provide structural support: Rasa, Rakta,
Mamsa, Medha, Asthi, Majja, and Sukra. While these tissues are essential
for structure, Ayurveda also recognizes three biological
systems---Vatha, Pitha, and Kapha---whose proper functioning guarantees
effective nourishment and vitality. When these systems are out of
balance, it leads to Dhatuvaishamya (disruption), resulting in various
health issues. In simple terms, when doshas are unbalanced, it leads to
tissue dysfunction.

Everyone has a unique combination of physical, mental, and emotional
traits, which is their constitution, shaped by numerous internal and
external factors like diet, climate, emotional tendencies, work demands,
and family dynamics. Straying from one\'s natural constitution indicates
disease or dysfunction. Ayurvedic treatment aims to restore this balance
through herbal remedies, dietary changes, physical therapies, and
spiritual-psychological healing. Following diet and lifestyle practices
tailored to individual constitution boosts health, mindset, and
consciousness.

**Vatha** (wind energy) is about subtle movement, consisting of air and
space. It governs processes like breathing, muscle actions, heart
rhythm, and cell activity. When balanced, Vatha fosters creativity and
adaptability; when it\'s off, it can lead to fear and anxiety.
Disruption often shows up as gas or muscle stiffness, causing
discomfort.

**Kapha** (phlegm energy) serves as the body's structure, comprising
earth and water elements. It keeps all systems hydrated, lubricates
joints, moisturizes the skin, and supports immune health. Balanced Kapha
brings love, tranquility, and compassion; imbalanced Kapha can lead to
greed and jealousy. When Kapha is disrupted, it often results in excess
fluid and mucus, leading to swelling.

**Pitha** (bile energy) is the body\'s metabolic fire, made up of water
and fire elements. It regulates digestion, nutrient absorption,
metabolism, and temperature. When balanced, Pitha nourishes intelligence
and comprehension; when it\'s unbalanced, it can lead to anger and
hostility. Pitha disruption often results in excess acidity and bile,
causing inflammation.

**Rajas** signifies motion and stimulation; all cravings and ambitions
arise from this quality. Many psychological issues can stem from an
imbalance in Rajas.

**Satva** represents lightness, awareness, and clarity of mind,
remaining free of disease. It\'s essential for perception, enabling
knowledge gain.

**Tamas** represents heaviness and inertia, which can interfere with
perception and mental function. Confusion, misunderstandings, lethargy,
and excessive sleep can all result from tamasic influence.

**Rasa** (Plasma tissue) comes from digested nutrients, fueling every
cell and tissue system.

**Raktha** (Blood tissue) is crucial for nourishing body tissues and
providing physical vitality.

**Mamsa** (Muscle tissue) gives physical strength and supports fat
structures.

**Meda** (Adipose tissue) supports the skeletal system.

**Asthi** (Bone tissue) includes the skeletal framework and cartilage,
providing a base for muscles.

**Majja** (Bone Marrow) fills the cavities in bones and lubricates the
body.

**Sukra** (Reproductive tissue) aids reproduction and boosts body
strength.

Ayurvedic theory posits that an imbalance in Vatha, Pitha, and Kapha
leads to disease. Assessing patients involves exploring disease
causation (nidana), early symptoms (purvaroopa), primary manifestations
(roopa), therapeutic exploration (upasaya), disease progression
(samprapthi), and clinical investigations. Evaluations may use Tenfold
assessment (Dasavidha Pareeksha) or Eightfold assessment (Ashtasthana
Pareeksha), which are particularly helpful in assessing both patient and
disease strength.

### **Tenfold Assessment (Dasavidha Pareeksha)**

This consists of: constitutional type (Prakriti), pathological condition
(Vikruthi), tissue quality (Sara), physical structure (Samhanana), body
proportions (Pramana), adaptability (Satmya), mental constitution
(Satva), digestive capacity (Aharasakthi), exercise tolerance
(Vyayamasakthi), and age (Vayas).

### **Eightfold Assessment (Ashtasthana Pareeksha)**

This includes examining: pulse (Nadi), tongue (Jihwa), voice (Sabda),
skin (Sparsa), vision (Drik), overall appearance (Akruthi), urine
(Muthra), and stool (Mala).

## **Ayurvedic Medicinal Preparations**

Ayurvedic medicines mainly use herbs and plant combinations, following
traditional pharmacological methods outlined in ancient texts. Forms
include:

**Swarasa** -- Fresh plant juice, with or without water added.

**Kalka** -- A paste made from ground herbs, sometimes soaked in water.

**Kashaya/Decoction** -- Coarsely ground herbs boiled in specific water
ratios until reduced.

**Choorna** -- Finely powdered single or compound herbs.

**Vati/Gulika** -- Tablets or pills from various herbs.

**Asava** -- Fermented herbal mixtures, filtered after soaking.

**Arishta** -- Similar to asava but involves decoctions for longer shelf
life.

**Avaleha** -- Semi-solid preparations made with sugar or jaggery.

**Thaila** -- Oil preparations with herbs boiled in specific oils.

**Ghritha** -- Similar to Thaila, but using ghee as the base.

**Lepa** -- Herbal pastes for external application.

**Bhasma** -- Calcinated mineral-herbal preparations.

These represent the commonly utilized forms in Ayurvedic medicine.

## **Four Essential Elements for Disease Cure**

Classical Ayurvedic texts highlight four key components for recovery:
skilled Physicians, effective Medicaments, proficient Nursing support,
and cooperative patients.

**Physician Qualities:** Comprehensive knowledge, proper medical
education, rigorous theoretical and practical training, and extensive
clinical experience, combined with physical and mental cleanliness.

**Medication Qualities:** Adequate stock, suitability for the condition,
diverse forms, and therapeutic strength.

**Nursing Qualities:** Rational mindset, nursing knowledge, technical
skill, compassion, cleanliness, and mental and physical purity.

**Patient Qualities:** Compliance with physician instructions, bravery,
and openness about symptoms.

## **Nutritional Approach**

In Ayurveda, food is seen as essential to life. Proper digestion,
influenced by digestive fire (jataragni)---gastric secretions and
enzymes---fuels the creation of seven life-sustaining tissues
(sapthadhatus). We tailor dietary strategies based on individual
constitution, focusing on digestive capacity rather than just calorie
counting. Each person\'s dosha predominance can change with time and
space, which affects dietary guidance. Bad food combinations can lead to
issues.

Dietary mistakes are a major cause of disease. Following the dietary
guidelines given by physicians is essential for patients and is a key
aspect of treatment, called Pathya in Ayurvedic terms. Food strengthens
the body, maintains body heat, and balances humors, all of which support
health and longevity. There are various taste categories: sweet, sour,
salty, bitter, pungent, and astringent.

Sweet foods boost fat tissue, strengthen structures, promote health, and
support longevity. Sour foods enhance appetite and digestion, have
cooling properties, but can generate heat after digestion, so moderation
is key. Salty foods aid elimination, help digestion, and increase
appetite. Bitter foods lighten digestion. Overuse of salt and bitter
foods can disrupt the balance of humors. Pungent foods promote appetite
but too much can lead to thirst and weakness. Astringent foods cool the
system, but long-term use can cause constipation.

We serve pure vegetarian meals from our kitchen, carefully considering
each individual's dietary needs based on location, climate, available
foods, and digestive capability. What works for one might not work for
another, so our doctors prescribe personalized diets aligned with health
needs, adjusting as treatment progresses.

Ayurveda advises daily living practices called Dinacharya, outlining
routines from morning to bedtime. Ignoring natural laws and virtuous
living leads to health problems.

### **Daily Routine (Dinacharya)**

**Morning Awakening:** It's advised to get up before sunrise. After
morning prayers, attend to natural needs while facing north. Then, brush
teeth thoroughly, rinse the mouth, eyes, and face with water, and
occasionally trim nails and hair for longevity, purity, and mental
peace.

**Bathing:** Cold baths help remove impurities, heat, and surface
irritation, purifying blood. Warm herbal baths have more general or
localized benefits. Steam baths can ease pain. Patients also receive oil
massages before relaxing on wooden benches. Applying oil benefits the
system deeply.

**Attire & Environment:** In winter, wear warm, silk clothes; in hot
weather, light and cool garments are best. For cold or rainy weather,
medium-weight clothing is ideal. Clean living spaces and well-ventilated
rooms are equally important.

**Rest & Activity:** Getting enough good-quality sleep in comfortable
spaces maintains health and improves complexion and strength. Daytime
sleep is generally discouraged. Ayurveda recommends physical activities
to boost strength and keep the body fit. Morning or evening walks are
better for those who consume richer foods.

## **Traditional Therapeutic Procedures from India**

### **Preparatory Treatments (Poorva Karma)**

Preparatory treatments are essential for getting patients ready for
Panchakarma. Snehana (oleation) and Swedana (sudation) are two key
preparatory steps. Snehana can be done through internal (oral) or
external means, while Swedana includes techniques like Kizhi, Pizhichil,
Dhara, and steam baths among others.

### **Panchakarma**

Panchakarma is a unique purification process with five therapies
designed to completely detoxify the body. This cleansing consists of
Emesis (Vamana), Purgation (Virechana), Enema (Vasthi), Nasal
administration (Nasya), and Bloodletting (Rakthamokshana). It follows
three main stages: Poorvakarma (preparatory treatments), Pradhanakarma
(primary treatment), and Paschathkarma (post-treatment care).

### **Abhyanga**

Abhyanga is about massaging the body with medicated oils using gentle
pressure. This time-honored therapy involves a warm oil massage covering
the whole body. Thaila/Ghritha/Vasa preparations are used in comfortable
motions to enhance strength and pacify Vatha. Both therapeutic and
preventive in nature, it's a significant practice.

Patients relax on treatment tables with their legs extended. Warm oil is
applied uniformly to the head, ears, and feet, with gentle pressure as
therapists work from both sides of the table. The treatment involves
various positions, ending with oil removal using tissues or towels,
followed by a hot water bath with medicated powders or soaps to cleanse
away any remaining oil. Light semi-solid meals follow bathing.

This ancient massage helps ease body stiffness and pain, allowing for
smoother movements by eliminating metabolic waste, enhancing blood flow,
and alleviating fatigue and stress-related issues. Our facility offers
Abhyanga for both preventive wellness and therapeutic purposes.

### **Pizhichil**

Pizhichil is a specialized form of Parisheka Sweda, unique to regional
Ayurvedic traditions. This treatment promotes perspiration by pouring
warm medicated oil in specific patterns, achieving both Snehana and
Swedana effects at once.

Patients sit on treatment tables with their legs outstretched.
Therapists on both sides perform gentle massages while oil heated in
water baths is poured over the body at a comfortable temperature. Cloths
dipped in warm oil are squeezed over the body while simultaneously being
massaged. The oil flows consistently downward from specific heights, and
the procedure continues through different positions, ending with gentle
massage and removal of oil with clean towels. A brief rest is followed
by hot water bathing and application of medicated powder. Normal diets
are typically prescribed. This method stimulates the neuromuscular
system, boosts circulation, enhances skin and sensory function, and
nourishes the body.

### **Shirodhara**

Shirodhara involves the continuous pouring of medicated oil or liquid
over the forehead, allowing a flow across the scalp from specific
heights for set periods. Patients may receive a head massage with
appropriate oil beforehand, sometimes along with a full-body massage.

Patients lie back on treatment tables with neck support while a
Shirodhara apparatus is adjusted overhead. Warm oil or liquids fall in
steady streams from the right heights on the forehead. The poured liquid
is collected, re-heated slightly above body temperature, and sent back
to the vessel. Attendants move the vessels side-to-side to ensure proper
coverage. After treatment, the oil is removed, and patients bathe with
medicated warm water after around thirty minutes. Treatment can last
from 7 to 21 days, depending on severity. Light diets, preferably
semi-solids and liquids, are recommended. The constant flow of liquid
relaxes and calms the mind.

### **Upanaham**

Upanaha is a sudation technique where a herbal paste, made by boiling
herbs with suitable liquids, is applied to specific areas and then
wrapped with leaves and thick cloth for a set time.

Warm oil is applied before the Upanaha treatment begins. A warm herbal
paste is then carefully applied to the treatment area. This is covered
and bandaged, with the paste removed after a designated duration,
followed by an oil application. Regular diets are usually advised. This
method provides localized heat, alleviating swelling and pain, while
improving circulation and mobilizing waste.

### **Kizhi Treatments**

**Pathra Pinda Sweda/Elakizhi**

This sudation uses specially prepared medicinal leaf packets. Leaves are
chopped, fried with rock salt and medicated oil, then tied in cotton
cloth as boluses. The boluses are gently applied to skin that's already
coated with warm medicated oil, keeping the heat maintained through
intermittent heating. After treatment, the body is wiped with dry towels
and oral medicines administered. Patients should rest for at least an
hour before bathing with warm water. Light liquid and semi-solid diets
are recommended.

This method combines heat, pressure, and medicated oil, stimulating and
strengthening the neuromuscular system while relieving pain and
inflammation. Elakizhi is effective in detoxifying skin areas, making it
suitable for those with back pain, joint issues, muscle cramps, and
anxiety, while also boosting immunity and slowing aging.

**Churna Pinda Sweda/Podikizhi**

This method uses powdered herb packets applied after heating. Plant
powders, seeds, lemon, and rock salt are mixed and fried with medicated
oil, then tied in cotton cloth as boluses, heated in oil, and applied
with gentle pressure after a light massage.

After treatment, the body is wiped with dry towels, oral medications
given, and patients rest before bathing with warm water. Light diets of
liquid and semi-solid foods are suggested. This treatment stimulates
nerve endings, relaxes muscles, and alleviates pain.

**Njavarakizhi**

This specialized treatment uses heated Njavara rice pudding boluses
applied externally to induce perspiration. Special Njavara rice is
cooked with milk and herbal decoction, then prepared in cloth packets.
Another decoction-milk mixture is warmed for bolus heating.

Patients receive a warm oil massage on their bodies and heads. At the
same time, the warm boluses are applied by two therapists on either
side. The heat is maintained comfortably, switching between four boluses
as needed. After treatment, the paste is scraped off, the body is wiped
with dry towels, and medicated oil is applied, followed by oral
medicine. Patients rest before bathing with warm water, and light
vegetarian liquid or semi-solid diets are recommended. This procedure
offers a blend of massage, heat, and pressure that nourishes muscles and
nerves.

### **Shirolepana**

This involves applying a medicinal paste to the head for specific
durations. Patients' heads are shaved, medicated oil is applied to their
body and scalp, followed by thick medicated paste and covered with
medicinal leaves. After the set time, the heads are washed, and
medicated powder is applied. Regular diets are advised. This promotes
cranial tranquility, soothes the mind, and nourishes brain cells, also
proving effective for mental retardation, memory loss, and psychiatric
conditions.

### **Shirovasthi**

Shirovasti retains medicated oil on the head using a cap for a specified
time. Medicated oil, heated slightly above body temperature, is poured
into the cap, filling it to a comfortable height above the scalp. The
temperature is kept consistent with regular replacements of warm oil.
Once finished, oil is removed, and the head is washed. Afterward,
patients may receive gentle massages on shoulders and neck as per the
physician\'s recommendation. Light liquid and semi-solid diets are
preferred.

### **Kativasthi**

Kativasthi retains warm medicated oil over the lower back using cap-like
devices for a set time. Patients undergo full-body Abhyanga followed by
medicated steam applied to the lower back and legs. Warm oil is poured
slowly from heated baths, maintaining a uniform temperature through
replacements. After the designated time, oil is wiped off and cleaned
with lukewarm water. Patients rest afterward, and a normal diet is
typically suggested. This oil retention nourishes nerves, muscles, and
joints in the area, providing soothing warmth and relief from symptoms.

### **Uro Vasthi**

Uro Vasthi involves keeping warm medicated oil on specific areas of the
chest for designated periods. A dough trough is formed on the chest or
heart area, where the oil is allowed to fill and remain for a while.
Once done, the oil is cleaned off, and patients rest afterward.
Treatments usually last around 7 days with a normal diet recommended.
Retaining the oil fosters nourishment and support in the targeted areas.

### **Akshi Tharpana**

In this process, medicated ghee is kept over the eyes for 15-20 minutes
while a gentle massage is given to the head, eyes, and face. Dough
troughs are placed around each eye, and warm medicated ghee is poured
inside for the duration. Afterward, the ghee is collected, and the face
is wiped clean.

Eyes may be covered with rose or lotus petals, and patients should avoid
sunlight for a day. This treatment can range from 7 to 14 days,
nourishing the ocular structures and strengthening the optic nerves.

### **Post-Treatment Protocol (Paschath Karma)**

After treatment, patients are advised to rest for an amount of time
equivalent to their therapy duration. Paschath Karma helps patients
regain their strength and return to normal. During this phase, they
should use warm water for all purposes and maintain celibacy. Daytime
naps should be avoided, but proper sleep at night is essential. Natural
urges shouldn't be suppressed, and patients should steer clear of
physical and mental exertion, as well as extreme temperatures and
environments. Moreover, prolonged sitting, loud speaking, and travel
should be limited. Soft pillows are recommended, and internal medicines
should be taken as prescribed to maximize effectiveness.
